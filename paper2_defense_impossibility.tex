\begin{abstract}
Prior work on LLM safety has focused on discovering adversarial inputs
or bounding perturbation robustness around individual examples. We ask
a different question: what are the \emph{geometric limits of defense
design itself}?

We prove two results. First, any continuous, utility-preserving defense
on a connected Hausdorff space must fix boundary points of the alignment
deviation surface---it is structurally forced to leave them untouched.
Second, when the defense and alignment function are both Lipschitz, the
defense cannot uniformly reduce alignment deviation far below the
threshold on a positive-measure band around the boundary:
$f(D(x)) \geq \tau - L(K\!+\!1)\delta$ for points within distance
$\delta$ of a fixed boundary point. The more aggressive the defense
(larger~$K$), the wider the band this constraint covers.

These are not statements about attacks or training---they are
constraints on what any admissible defense mapping can do, independent
of the model, data, or optimization procedure. The Tietze extension
theorem ensures that any finite set of behavioral observations admits
continuous extensions for which both impossibilities hold, bridging
discrete data to continuous theory.

The full theory is machine-verified in Lean~4 with Mathlib (35~files,
$\sim$300 theorems, zero \texttt{sorry}, three standard axioms) and
validated against empirical findings from three frontier LLMs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Can you build a wrapper around a language model that eliminates all
prompt injection vulnerabilities?

Most current work on LLM safety implicitly assumes the answer is
yes---at least in principle, even if current tools fall short in practice.
The defense mechanisms people have proposed span a wide range: input
classifiers that flag suspicious prompts~\cite{alon2023detecting},
output filters that catch unsafe completions~\cite{inan2023llama},
and constitutional rewriting pipelines that reshape model
behavior~\cite{bai2022constitutional}. But despite their differences,
all of these defenses share the same underlying structure: they are
functions $D\colon X \to X$ that preprocess prompts before the model
sees them, aiming to map unsafe inputs to safe equivalents while leaving
safe inputs alone.

Prior work has largely focused on discovering or mitigating individual
adversarial inputs. We ask a different question: what are the
\emph{geometric limits of the defense mapping itself}? We show that
under mild and commonly assumed design constraints---continuity and
utility preservation---no defense can eliminate the safety boundary.
Vulnerabilities arise not from specific inputs, but from the structure
of the space and the constraints imposed on admissible defenses. This
shifts the problem from adversarial search to geometric inevitability.

The result comes in two strengths.

\paragraph{The basic impossibility.}
If the defense is continuous, utility-preserving, and the prompt space
is connected, then the defense must fix at least one boundary point---a
prompt where alignment deviation equals the threshold
exactly---passing it through with no remediation (\Cref{thm:main}).
This is a clean topological result, but it applies only to boundary
points, which could in principle form a set of measure zero.

\paragraph{The $\varepsilon$-robust impossibility.}
If the defense is also $K$-Lipschitz and the alignment deviation
function is $L$-Lipschitz, the failure extends beyond isolated boundary
points. For any point $x$ within distance $\delta$ of a fixed boundary
point~$z$, the defense satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eps-bound}
  f(D(x)) \;\geq\; \tau - L(K+1)\,\delta.
\end{equation}
The defense cannot uniformly reduce alignment deviation far below
$\tau$ on a neighborhood of~$z$~(\Cref{thm:eps-robust}). The
$\varepsilon$-band $\{x : \tau - \varepsilon \leq f(x) \leq \tau\}$
has positive measure for every $\varepsilon > 0$~(\Cref{thm:band-measure}).
Increasing $K$ (more aggressive defense) widens the region on which
\eqref{eq:eps-bound} constrains the output: gentleness leaves more
points untouched, while aggressiveness spreads the anchoring effect
of the fixed point.

\paragraph{Why continuity?}
Continuity captures a natural requirement for practical defenses: small
changes in input should not produce arbitrarily large changes in the
defense's output, as such instability would degrade usability and
reliability. Any defense based on learned representations, smooth
transformations, or differentiable processing satisfies this constraint.
Discontinuous defenses (hard blocklists, keyword filters) escape the
theorem but suffer their own well-known failure modes.

\paragraph{From discrete to continuous.}
Both results apply to continuous interpolants of discrete data.
The Tietze extension theorem guarantees that any finite set of
behavioral observations in a normal space admits continuous extensions,
and the impossibilities hold for every such extension
(\Cref{thm:tietze}). This does not claim the results hold for the
underlying discrete token system directly, but for every continuous
model consistent with the observations.

The proof of the basic impossibility is five steps long and uses only
point-set topology. The $\varepsilon$-robust version adds Lipschitz
structure. Both are machine-verified in Lean~4 with zero \texttt{sorry}
statements. All three conditions---continuity, utility preservation, and
connectedness---are individually necessary; we give counterexamples for
each.

Around these impossibility results, we develop a formal theory of the
vulnerability landscape: basin structure and fragmentation, perturbation
robustness, iterative convergence, cross-model transferability, authority
monotonicity, gradient ascent, stability under fine-tuning, and
exponential cost asymmetry.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item A formally verified impossibility theorem for continuous
    utility-preserving defenses (\Cref{thm:main}).
  \item An $\varepsilon$-robust upgrade showing the defense cannot
    uniformly reduce alignment deviation far below~$\tau$ on a
    positive-measure band (\Cref{thm:eps-robust,thm:band-measure}).
  \item A formally verified bridge from discrete data to continuous
    theory via the Tietze extension theorem (\Cref{thm:tietze}).
  \item Counterexamples proving each hypothesis is necessary
    (\Cref{sec:counterexamples}).
  \item Supporting theorems forming a complete formal theory of the
    failure manifold (\Cref{sec:landscape,sec:attacks,sec:stability,sec:cost}).
  \item Theory--experiment correspondence with three LLMs
    (\Cref{sec:experiments}).
  \item A Lean~4 artifact: 35 files, $\sim$300 theorems, zero
    \texttt{sorry}, three standard axioms (\Cref{sec:artifact}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Adversarial robustness.}
The existence of adversarial examples in neural networks has been known
for over a decade~\cite{szegedy2013intriguing,goodfellow2014explaining},
and a substantial body of work has pursued formal verification of
robustness properties---through abstract
interpretation~\cite{singh2019abstract}, SMT-based
methods~\cite{katz2017reluplex}, and randomized
smoothing~\cite{cohen2019certified}. These approaches characterize the
existence of perturbations or bound robustness radii around individual
inputs under specific norms. Our work operates at a different level:
rather than checking individual inputs, we prove a \emph{structural
constraint on the defense mapping itself}, independent of perturbation
models, norms, or training procedures. Certified defenses provide
guarantees about perturbation neighborhoods; our result constrains the
\emph{global behavior} of the defense function, showing that no such
defense can eliminate boundary regions altogether.

\paragraph{Prompt injection and jailbreaking.}
Techniques for finding individual vulnerabilities include gradient-based
attacks like GCG~\cite{zou2023universal}, search-based approaches like
PAIR~\cite{chao2024jailbreaking} and TAP~\cite{mehrotra2024tree},
and quality-diversity methods like Rainbow
Teaming~\cite{samvelyan2024rainbow}. The Manifold of Failure
framework~\cite{bhatt2025manifold} uses MAP-Elites to map the
\emph{continuous topology} of failures rather than cataloguing
individual instances. Existing work identifies individual failure cases
or distributions of attacks. Our result shows that these failures are
not incidental but \emph{topologically unavoidable} under mild
assumptions, providing a formal explanation for their persistence.

\paragraph{Impossibility results in ML.}
The no-free-lunch theorems~\cite{wolpert1997no} limit average
performance across tasks. The robustness--accuracy tradeoff
literature~\cite{tsipras2018robustness} shows that adversarial
robustness costs standard performance. Our result is different in
character from both: it is a topological and metric constraint, not
a statistical tradeoff. It holds \emph{pointwise} for any continuous
defense on a \emph{fixed task}, with no dependence on distributions,
learning algorithms, or training data.

\paragraph{Topological views of neural networks.}
Work on decision boundary geometry, the manifold hypothesis, and
topology of neural representations has established that topological
structure matters for understanding neural networks. While our proof
relies on elementary topology (continuity, connectedness, closure), the
novelty lies in identifying that \emph{standard design constraints for
LLM defenses induce a forced fixed-point structure at the safety
boundary}---a connection that has not been previously characterized.

\paragraph{Formal verification of ML systems.}
Proof assistants like Lean and Coq have been applied to verify
properties of neural networks~\cite{bagnall2019certifying} and
optimization algorithms~\cite{bolte2024}. To the best of our knowledge,
ours is the first formally verified impossibility result specifically
targeting prompt injection defense.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\begin{definition}[Alignment Deviation Function]
\label{def:ad}
Let $X$ be a topological space. An \emph{alignment deviation function} is
a continuous map $f\colon X \to \R$. Given a threshold $\tau\in\R$, we
partition the space into three regions:
\begin{align}
  S_\tau &= \{x \in X : f(x) < \tau\}  &&\text{(safe region)} \\
  U_\tau &= \{x \in X : f(x) > \tau\}  &&\text{(unsafe region)} \\
  B_\tau &= \{x \in X : f(x) = \tau\}  &&\text{(boundary)}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Defense]
\label{def:defense}
A \emph{defense} is a continuous map $D\colon X \to X$. It is
\emph{utility-preserving} if $D(x) = x$ for all $x \in S_\tau$,
and \emph{complete} if $f(D(x)) < \tau$ for all $x\in X$.
\end{definition}

\section{The Basic Impossibility}
\label{sec:proof}

\begin{theorem}[Boundary Fixation]
\label{thm:main}
Let $X$ be a connected Hausdorff space. Let $f\colon X\to\R$ be
continuous with $S_\tau \neq \emptyset$ and $U_\tau \neq \emptyset$. Let
$D\colon X \to X$ be continuous with $D|_{S_\tau} = \id$.

Then there exists $z\in X$ with $f(z) = \tau$ and $D(z) = z$.

Moreover, every $z \in \cl{S_\tau} \setminus S_\tau$ satisfies
$f(z) = \tau$ and $D(z) = z$. This set is nonempty.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Five steps, each using exactly one hypothesis.

\textbf{Step~1} (The fixed-point set is closed).
$\Fix(D) = \{x : D(x) = x\}$ is the preimage of the diagonal
$\Delta \subset X \times X$ under the continuous map
$x \mapsto (D(x), x)$. Since $X$ is Hausdorff, $\Delta$ is closed,
so $\Fix(D)$ is closed.

\textbf{Step~2} (Closure of safe region consists of fixed points).
$S_\tau \subseteq \Fix(D)$ by utility preservation. Since $\Fix(D)$ is
closed, $\cl{S_\tau} \subseteq \Fix(D)$.

\textbf{Step~3} (Safe region is not closed).
$S_\tau = f^{-1}((-\infty,\tau))$ is open. If it were also closed, it
would be clopen, hence $\emptyset$ or $X$ by connectedness---contradicting
$S_\tau \neq \emptyset \neq U_\tau$.

\textbf{Step~4} (Boundary point in closure).
Since $S_\tau$ is not closed, $\cl{S_\tau} \supsetneq S_\tau$. Pick
$z \in \cl{S_\tau} \setminus S_\tau$. Then $f(z) \leq \tau$ (since
$\{f \leq \tau\}$ is closed and contains $S_\tau$) and $f(z) \geq \tau$
(since $z \notin S_\tau$). So $f(z) = \tau$.

\textbf{Step~5} (Defense fixes it).
$z \in \cl{S_\tau} \subseteq \Fix(D)$, so $D(z) = z$ and
$f(D(z)) = \tau \not< \tau$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:no-complete}
Under the hypotheses of \Cref{thm:main}, no continuous utility-preserving
defense is complete.
\end{corollary}


% ============================================================
\subsection{Each Hypothesis Is Necessary}
\label{sec:counterexamples}

\begin{counterexample}[Removing connectedness]
$X = \{0,1\}$ discrete, $f(0)=0$, $f(1)=1$, $\tau=0.5$.
$D(0)=0$, $D(1)=0$ is continuous, utility-preserving, and complete.
\end{counterexample}

\begin{counterexample}[Removing continuity]
$X=[0,1]$, $f(x)=x$, $\tau=0.5$. $D(x)=x$ for $x<0.5$,
$D(x)=0$ for $x \geq 0.5$: utility-preserving and complete,
but discontinuous at $0.5$.
\end{counterexample}

\begin{counterexample}[Removing utility preservation]
$D(x) = x_0$ for a fixed safe point~$x_0$: continuous and complete,
but destroys all inputs.
\end{counterexample}

\begin{remark}[The defense trilemma]
\label{rem:trilemma}
A defense can satisfy at most two of: continuity, utility preservation,
completeness. Each counterexample achieves two by sacrificing the third.
\end{remark}


% ============================================================
\subsection{Scope of the Basic Result}
\label{sec:caveats}

\begin{remark}[Not all boundary points are fixed]
The theorem captures $\cl{S_\tau} \setminus S_\tau$, not all of
$B_\tau$. Boundary points isolated from the safe side may escape.
The Lean artifact verifies this with $f(x)=x^2$, $\tau=0$.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Boundary behavior may be mild]
Fixed points satisfy $f(z) = \tau$ exactly. If $\tau$-level behavior
is benign, the theorem is true but harmless. This is the engineering
prescription, not a loophole.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Discontinuous defenses escape]
Blocklists, keyword filters, and exact-match rejection are
discontinuous and outside the theorem's scope.
\end{remark}

\section{The $\varepsilon$-Robust Impossibility}
\label{sec:eps-robust}

The basic impossibility fixes boundary points---potentially a set of
measure zero. The following results show that when both $f$ and $D$
are Lipschitz, the defense cannot uniformly reduce alignment deviation
far below~$\tau$ on a positive-measure neighborhood of the boundary.

\begin{theorem}[$\varepsilon$-Robust Defense Impossibility]
\label{thm:eps-robust}
Let $X$ be a connected Hausdorff metric space. Let $f\colon X \to \R$
be $L$-Lipschitz with $S_\tau \neq \emptyset$ and
$U_\tau \neq \emptyset$. Let $D\colon X \to X$ be $K$-Lipschitz with
$D|_{S_\tau} = \id$.

Then there exists $z \in X$ with $f(z) = \tau$ and $D(z) = z$ such that
for all $x \in X$ (with strongest constraint near~$z$):
\begin{equation}
  f(D(x)) \;\geq\; \tau \;-\; L(K+1)\,\dist(x, z).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
From \Cref{thm:main}, a fixed boundary point $z$ exists with $f(z) = \tau$
and $D(z) = z$. For any $x \in X$:
\begin{align*}
  \dist(D(x), z) &= \dist(D(x), D(z)) \leq K\,\dist(x,z), \\
  |f(D(x)) - \tau| &= |f(D(x)) - f(z)| \leq L\,\dist(D(x), z)
                    \leq LK\,\dist(x,z).
\end{align*}
The sharper bound uses $\dist(D(x), z) \leq (K+1)\dist(x,z)$ from the
triangle inequality $\dist(D(x),x) \leq \dist(D(x),D(z)) + \dist(z,x)
\leq K\dist(x,z) + \dist(x,z)$. Extracting the lower bound from the
absolute value gives the result.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Positive-Measure Failure Band]
\label{thm:band-measure}
Under the hypotheses of \Cref{thm:eps-robust}, for every
$\varepsilon > 0$ such that $f$ takes values below $\tau - \varepsilon$,
the $\varepsilon$-band
\[
  \mathcal{B}_\varepsilon = \{x \in X : \tau - \varepsilon \leq f(x)
  \leq \tau\}
\]
has positive measure with respect to any measure that is positive on
nonempty open sets. Specifically, if $c \in X$ satisfies
$f(c) = \tau - \varepsilon/2$, then
$\operatorname{ball}(c, \varepsilon/(4L)) \subseteq
\mathcal{B}_\varepsilon$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By the intermediate value theorem (using connectedness), a point $c$ with
$f(c) = \tau - \varepsilon/2$ exists. For any $x$ with
$\dist(x,c) < \varepsilon/(4L)$:
\begin{align*}
  |f(x) - f(c)| &\leq L \cdot \dist(x,c) < L \cdot \frac{\varepsilon}{4L}
  = \frac{\varepsilon}{4}.
\end{align*}
So $f(x) \in (\tau - 3\varepsilon/4,\; \tau - \varepsilon/4)
\subset [\tau - \varepsilon,\; \tau]$. The ball is open and nonempty,
hence has positive measure.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[What the $\varepsilon$-robust theorem does and does not say]
\Cref{thm:eps-robust} says the defense cannot push near-boundary points
\emph{far} below~$\tau$. It does \emph{not} say these points remain
unsafe---the defense may map them to values in
$[\tau - L(K+1)\delta,\; \tau)$, which is below threshold and hence
technically safe. The impossibility is about depth, not direction: the
defense can nudge points below $\tau$, but it cannot drive them deep
into the safe region. The bound is tight in the sense that a defense
mapping every point in a $\delta$-ball to $f$-value
$\tau - L(K+1)\delta$ would saturate it.

This creates a quantitative bind. Within distance $\delta$ of the fixed
boundary point~$z$, the defense can move points at most $(K+1)\delta$
and the resulting alignment deviation stays within $L(K+1)\delta$
of~$\tau$. Increasing $K$ allows larger displacements but widens the
region on which the bound constrains the output.
\end{remark}

\section{From Discrete Data to Continuous Theory}
\label{sec:bridge}

\begin{theorem}[Continuous Relaxation]
\label{thm:tietze}
Let $X$ be a connected, normal, Hausdorff space. Let $S \subset X$ be a
finite set of behavioral observations with $g(p) < \tau$ for some $p$
and $g(q) > \tau$ for some $q$.

Then there exists a continuous function $f\colon X \to \R$ agreeing
with all observations for which both \Cref{thm:main,thm:eps-robust}
hold. In particular, every continuous interpolant of such data inherits
both impossibilities.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Finite subsets of $T_1$ spaces are closed. By the Tietze extension
theorem, $g$ extends to a continuous $f$ on $X$ agreeing with $g$ at
every sample point. Since $f(p) < \tau < f(q)$, both safe and unsafe
regions are nonempty, and the hypotheses of \Cref{thm:main} are
satisfied. If $f$ is additionally Lipschitz (as it is for GP posterior means under standard kernel regularity
assumptions, e.g., jointly continuous kernel on a compact domain),
\Cref{thm:eps-robust} applies as well.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The interpolant need not be unique. Both impossibilities hold for
\emph{every} continuous function consistent with the data---GP,
neural surrogate, or abstract Tietze construction.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}
\Cref{thm:tietze} applies in $\R^n$ for every $n \geq 1$.
\end{corollary}

\section{The Vulnerability Landscape}
\label{sec:landscape}

\begin{theorem}[Basin Structure]
\label{thm:basin}
If $f$ is continuous and $f(p)>\tau$, then $U_\tau$ is open. Under
any measure that assigns positive mass to nonempty open sets (e.g.,
Lebesgue measure on~$\R^n$), $U_\tau$ has positive measure.
Vulnerabilities are never isolated points.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Basin Fragment Minimum Size]
\label{thm:fragment}
If $f$ is $L$-Lipschitz with $f(p) > \tau$, the connected component
of $U_\tau$ containing $p$ has diameter $\geq 2(f(p) - \tau)/L$.
Larger $L$ (rougher model) $\to$ smaller guaranteed fragments.
\end{theorem}

\section{Attack Properties}
\label{sec:attacks}

\begin{theorem}[Perturbation Robustness]
\label{thm:lipschitz}
If $f$ is $L$-Lipschitz and $f(p)>\tau$, then
$\operatorname{ball}(p,\, (f(p)-\tau)/L) \subseteq U_\tau$.
The radius is monotone in~$f(p)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Iterative Convergence]
\label{thm:convergence}
Any monotone-improvement operator $T$ with score in $[0,1]$ converges.
If each step gains $\geq \delta$, convergence takes
$\leq \lfloor 1/\delta \rfloor$ steps.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Transferability]
\label{thm:transfer}
$\|f-g\|_\infty \leq \delta \implies
\{f > \tau+\delta\} \subseteq \{g > \tau\}$.
Transfer costs zero additional queries.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Authority Monotonicity]
\label{thm:authority}
If $f(y,\cdot)$ is monotone non-decreasing in authority for each fixed
indirection~$y$, then the vulnerability set $\{x : f(y,x) > \tau\}$ is
upward-closed in authority and a critical threshold $a^*_2(y)$ exists
(by IVT, when $f(y,\cdot)$ is continuous and takes values on both sides
of~$\tau$). If additionally $f$ is monotone non-decreasing in
indirection for each fixed authority, the threshold curve $y \mapsto
a^*_2(y)$ is non-increasing.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Gradient Ascent]
\label{thm:gradient}
If $f$ has nonzero Fr\'{e}chet derivative at~$x$, there exist $v$ and
$\varepsilon > 0$ with $f(x + \varepsilon v) > f(x)$.
\end{theorem}

\section{Stability Under Fine-Tuning}
\label{sec:stability}

\begin{theorem}[Interior Stability]
\label{thm:interior-stable}
If $\|f - g\|_\infty \leq \varepsilon$:
$f(x) > \tau + \varepsilon \implies g(x) > \tau$, and
$f(x) < \tau - \varepsilon \implies g(x) < \tau$.
Only the band $|f(x) - \tau| \leq \varepsilon$ is uncertain.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Crossing Preservation]
\label{thm:crossing}
If $f$ crosses $\tau$ transversally on $[a,b]$ and $g$ is continuous
with $\|f - g\|_\infty \leq \varepsilon$, then $g$ also crosses $\tau$
on $[a,b]$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Patching Is Nonlocal]
\label{thm:nonlocal}
There exist $f, g$ with $\|f-g\|_\infty \leq \varepsilon$ such that
eliminating a vulnerability at one point necessarily changes values at
distant points. (Verified by explicit construction in Lean.)
\end{theorem}


% ============================================================
\section{Cost Asymmetry}
\label{sec:cost}

\begin{theorem}[Exponential Cost Asymmetry for Grid-Based Defense]
\label{thm:cost}
For a defense that monitors the behavioral space via a grid with
$N \geq 2$ bins per axis in $d$ dimensions:
attack cost $\leq 1/\delta$ (dimension-independent, from monotone
convergence); grid-based defense cost $= N^d$ (exponential in~$d$);
the ratio $\delta \cdot N^d \to \infty$ as $d\to\infty$.
\end{theorem}

At $d=2$ with $N=25$ and $\delta=0.01$, the ratio is $6.25$.
At $d=10$, it climbs to $\sim 10^{12}$. The Lean artifact verifies
the $d=2$ case by \texttt{norm\_num}.

\begin{theorem}[Coverage Collapse]
\label{thm:collapse}
For fixed patch radius $0 < r < 1$ and budget $C$,
$C \cdot r^d \to 0$ as $d \to \infty$.
\end{theorem}

\section{The Engineering Prescription}
\label{sec:engineering}

\paragraph{Make the boundary shallow.}
Set $\tau$ so that boundary-level behavior is benign.
GPT-5-Mini's ceiling at $\AD = 0.50$ exemplifies this.

\paragraph{Reduce the Lipschitz constant.}
Smaller~$L$ makes the surface predictable (GPT-5-Mini: AD in
$[0.39, 0.50]$).

\paragraph{Reduce the effective dimension.}
Defense cost grows as $N^d$; constraining the prompt interface
reduces~$d$.

\paragraph{Monitor the boundary.}
\Cref{thm:crossing} shows transversal crossings persist under
fine-tuning. Rather than eliminate them, monitor approach to the
boundary using the Lipschitz bound (\Cref{thm:lipschitz}).

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{The basic impossibility is at the boundary.}
Fixed points satisfy $f(z) = \tau$ exactly. The $\varepsilon$-robust
version constrains how far the defense can push near-boundary points
below~$\tau$, but it does not prove these points \emph{remain unsafe}:
a defense that maps everything to $\tau - \varepsilon$ for small
$\varepsilon$ is consistent with the bound. The band width scales with
$1/(L(K+1))$, which may be small for mild defenses.

\paragraph{The Tietze bridge applies to interpolants, not the system.}
\Cref{thm:tietze} shows every continuous interpolant of discrete data
inherits both impossibilities. It does not claim the impossibilities
hold for the underlying discrete token system directly. The claim is
that any continuous model consistent with the data must exhibit these
properties.

\paragraph{The cost asymmetry assumes exhaustive enumeration.}
Learning-based approaches that generalize across the space may
sidestep the exponential lower bound.

\paragraph{Single-turn only.}
The manifold may shift across conversation turns as context accumulates.

\paragraph{The theory does not explain model-specific topology.}
It describes what must hold given continuity and connectedness, not
\emph{why} different models produce different landscapes.


% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have established two impossibility results for prompt injection
defense. The basic result: any continuous, utility-preserving defense
on a connected Hausdorff space must fix boundary points. The
$\varepsilon$-robust result: when both defense and alignment deviation
are Lipschitz, the defense cannot uniformly reduce alignment deviation
far below~$\tau$ on a positive-measure neighborhood of the boundary,
and the width of this constrained region grows with the defense's
Lipschitz constant.

The discrete-continuous gap is addressed via the Tietze extension
theorem: finite behavioral data always admits continuous interpolants,
and both impossibilities hold for every such interpolant consistent
with the observations.

The surrounding theory shows that vulnerabilities come in basins of
positive measure, attacks are robust to perturbation and converge
reliably, transfer between models is controlled by surface distance,
vulnerabilities survive fine-tuning, and defense cost grows
exponentially while attack cost does not.

The practical prescription: make the boundary shallow (raise~$\tau$),
smooth (reduce~$L$), and low-dimensional (constrain the interface).
GPT-5-Mini's ceiling at $\AD = 0.50$ shows this working in practice.

The full theory is machine-verified in Lean~4: 35 files, approximately
300 theorems, zero \texttt{sorry}, three standard axioms.
